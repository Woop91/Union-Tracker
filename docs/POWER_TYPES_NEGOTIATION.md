# Types of Power in Negotiation, Conflict Resolution & Union Contexts

## Structural Power

- **Positional Power** — Authority derived from role or title (e.g., management's right to direct work, a union president's authority to call a vote)
- **Institutional Power** — Power embedded in rules, laws, and contracts (e.g., NLRA protections, collective bargaining agreements, grievance procedures)
- **Resource Power** — Control over things others need: budgets, staffing, equipment, information

## Relational Power

- **Coalition Power** — Strength in numbers; the foundational power of unions. Solidarity, mutual aid, and collective action amplify individual voices
- **Network Power** — Access to key people, decision-makers, or allied organizations (other locals, community groups, political allies)
- **Referent Power** — Influence through trust, respect, and personal relationships. A steward who members trust carries more weight than one who simply holds the title

## Knowledge-Based Power

- **Expert Power** — Deep knowledge of the contract, labor law, past practice, or technical work processes. The steward who knows the CBA inside-out holds enormous power at the grievance table
- **Information Power** — Access to or control over critical data (staffing numbers, financials, incident records, precedent grievances)

## Economic/Leverage Power

- **Strike Power** — The ultimate union economic weapon; withholding labor
- **BATNA Power** (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) — The stronger your walkaway option, the more power you hold. A union with a healthy strike fund has a stronger BATNA
- **Market Power** — Scarcity of the skills workers provide; hard-to-replace workers have inherent leverage

## Tactical/Process Power

- **Agenda Power** — Controlling what gets discussed, in what order, and what gets tabled
- **Framing Power** — Defining the narrative. "Cost-saving measure" vs. "unsafe staffing levels" — whoever controls the frame shapes the outcome
- **Time/Deadline Power** — Using contract expiration dates, statutory timelines, or management's urgency to create pressure
- **Procedural Power** — Knowledge of and ability to use formal processes (grievance steps, arbitration, ULP filings, information requests under NLRA §8(a)(5))

## Moral/Narrative Power

- **Legitimacy Power** — Appeals to fairness, justice, past practice, or public standards. "The contract says..." or "Every other unit in the state gets..."
- **Public/Community Power** — Ability to shape public opinion, media coverage, or political support. Particularly effective in public-sector unions
- **Moral Authority** — Power that comes from being on the right side of an issue, especially when documented and evidence-based

## Personal/Psychological Power

- **Persistence Power** — The willingness to keep pushing a grievance through every step
- **Emotional Intelligence** — Reading the room, managing tension, knowing when to push and when to de-escalate
- **Confidence/Presence** — Projecting calm authority at the table, refusing to be intimidated

---

## How These Interact in Practice

In **grievance handling**, a steward typically combines *expert power* (contract knowledge), *information power* (documented facts), *institutional power* (the grievance procedure itself), and *legitimacy power* (past practice, just cause standards).

In **contract negotiations**, the union's primary leverage comes from *coalition power* (membership solidarity), *economic power* (credible strike threat), and *BATNA power* — all reinforced by *information power* (knowing the employer's financials and comparables).

In **conflict resolution**, the most effective approach often shifts from *positional power* toward *relational power* and *framing power* — finding shared interests and redefining the problem collaboratively.

The key insight: **power is not fixed**. It is situational, relational, and can be built. A union that lacks economic leverage can build coalition, narrative, and procedural power to compensate. The most effective advocates layer multiple types of power simultaneously.
